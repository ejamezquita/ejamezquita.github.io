1.1 Reminder of some mathematical concepts¶

Whenever we draw a sample of size $n$, we consider a set of $n$ measurements $x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n$. From there, we can compute its sample mean $\bar\mu$ (or $\bar x$) and its sample population standard deviation $\bar\sigma$ (or $s$). Notice the bars on top.

$$\bar\mu = \frac{x_1+x_2+\ldots+x_n}n=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^nx_i.$$

$$\bar\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{(x_1-\bar\mu)^2 +\ldots+ (x_n-\bar\mu)^2}{n-1}} = \sqrt{\frac1{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar\mu)^2}.$$

These are the formulas you and Python have always been using.

In real life, $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are unknown. We only have $\bar\mu$ and $\bar\sigma$ at our disposal. While similar, they are not the same. This distinction can have serious implications.¶

1.2 A note on Bessel's correction¶

In many textbooks you'll see that they use $\frac1n$ instead of $\frac1{n-1}$ for the sample standard deviation. We will use the $n-1$ formula, which is known as the Bessel's correction and produces better results.

Important: SciPy and pandas apply Bessel's correction by default. NumPy does not, but we can force it by specifying the ddof = 1 parameter.

2. The Law of Large Numbers (LLN)¶

For most of the datasets we have worked with so far, we take the mean of our experiment measurements as a first step to gauge how our data behaves. And means are not just for vibe checks. If you recall, whenever we have two different populations, the t-test is the check whether the means of these two populations are indeed statistically the same or not.

Think again of the hemp-fed cows and the 9-THC concentration in their livers:

The population mean $\mu$ would be the mean you get by measuring the livers of all the cows: past, present, and future, from Alaska to Patagonia. Obviously, it is impossible to calculate the exact $\mu$ value. We can only take a small number of cows and compute $\bar\mu$ instead.

Are $\mu$ and $\bar\mu$ close? Yes!

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN), in short, says that if we have larger and larger sample size (increasing $n$), then the sample mean will get closer and closer to the population mean.

2.1 The fine print¶

LLN says that if we keep increasing our sample size, our measured mean will eventually match the population mean as long as two important conditions hold.

  1. Independence: All samples must be independent from each other
    • For the THC concentration data, samples are independent because the cannabinoids going into one cow did not influence in any way the liver of another cow.
  2. Identical distribution: All samples must come from the same population
    • All the cows used in the experiment were the same subspecies, age, upbringing, etc.
    • In other words, all external factors were accounted for, making all the cows essentially identical.

In math textbooks these two conditions are referred to as IID: Independent and Identically Distributed.

3. The Central Limit Theorem, revisited¶

The LLN tells us that the sample mean gets closer to the population mean as we increase the sample size. We can take that idea one step further with the CLT: the sample means follow a normal distribution (regardless of the underlying distribution) which gets tighter as the sample size increases.

More specifically, if we have a sample of size $n$, the sample mean $\bar\mu$ distributes as a normal of mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$:

$$\bar\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma/\sqrt n).$$

  4. Make a histogram of these sample means

4. Confidence Intervals, revisited¶

In the previous class we saw that one way to compute the 95% Confidence Interval is through the formula $$CI = \bar\mu \pm z_\alpha\times\frac\sigma{\sqrt{n}},$$ Where $z_\alpha$ is the appropriate quantile from the normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

The formula is based on the CLT that says that $\bar\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma/\sqrt n)$. In other words, if we know the population mean and population standard deviation, we know how $\bar\mu$ behaves. But we don't know $\mu$ nor $\sigma$!

Thanks to the LLN, we can get away using the sample mean instead of the population mean. But we cannot have the same guarantees if we use the sample standard deviation instead of the population standard deviation. Especially if $n$ is small (which is often the case in biology-inspired experiments).

To substitute, perchance to deviate: ay, there's the rub!

ax[i].tick_params(bottom=False, labelbottom=False)
ax[i].set_ylim( max([0, mu - z*sigma]), mu + z*sigma)

inside, outside = 0,0
for j in range(1,1+N):
    k = 0.1*j
    # Draw a sample of size n
    sample = distr[key].rvs(n)

    # Get its sample mean and SD

    sample_sd = sample.std(ddof=1)

    # Use CI with the sample SD

    # Uncomment the line below using the population SD

    # Keep track on whether the interval contains indeed or not the population mean
    if (mu >= ci_lower) and (mu <= ci_upper):
        ax[i].plot([k,k], [ci_lower, ci_upper], c='dodgerblue', lw=2, zorder=3)
        inside += 1
    else:
        ax[i].plot([k,k], [ci_lower, ci_upper], c='gray', lw=2, zorder=3)

print('Intervals containing mu:\t{} [{:.1f}%]'.format(inside, inside/N*100))

5. The Student's t-distribution¶

From the simulation above, you might have observed that:

  • Whenever using the sample SD $\bar\sigma$ instead of the population SD $\sigma$, the confidence intervals miss the mean $\mu$ at larger rates than expected.
  • This error is aggravated if the data comes from a skewed distribution.
  • This error can be fixed by either:
    • Increasing the sample size $n$.
    • Using $\sigma$ and not $\bar\sigma$.

The moral of the story is: just collect a lot of data and you'll be fine. Case closed.

Except we cannot close the case.

  • In real life, we very rarely—if ever—know the population SD $\sigma$. So that "solution" is a non-starter. We only have the sample SD.
  • More often than not, due to time/money/energy constraints, the "go collect more data" is not an option.

Is there a third possible solution? YES—KIND OF..

5.1 The t-distribution to the rescue!¶

We cannot use $\sigma$ as the CLT demands because in real life we don't have that number. So far we have been computing the CIs as $$CI_\alpha = \bar\mu \pm z_\alpha\times\frac{\bar\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}, \quad\text{(this formula does not always work in real life)}$$ with $z_\alpha$ the appropriate quantile from a standard normal distribution.

If you look at the gray CIs in our prior simulations (CIs that fail to contain $\mu$), notice that many of them would turn blue if they were stretched a bit more. If we have a large sample size $n$, we just need a little stretch. But if we have a small $n$, the stretch has to be a tad more generous.

In other words, instead of $z_\alpha$, we need quantiles $t_\alpha$ from a distribution that is larger than normal when $n$ is small. But this same distribution is pretty much the normal when $n$ is large.

Such distribution is the Student's t !

The standard Student's $t$ is symmetric, centered at 0, but it depends of a parameter known as degrees of freedom—$df$ for short. You'll see that $df$ is closely related to sample size $n$.

5.3 Confidence Intervals, recomputed¶

We are finally able to check the revised formula for confidence intervals: $$CI_\alpha = \bar\mu\pm t_{\alpha, n-1}\times\frac{\bar\sigma}{\sqrt n}.$$ We are going to use the quantiles from a $t$ distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom instead of the normal.

  • As you have observed before, our CIs tend to be more inaccurate when we have small sample sizes $n$. We have the following logic:
  • Small sample size $n$
    • → small degree of freedom
      • → heavy tail for the $t$ distribution
        • → the $0.975$-th quantile value is much larger for $t$ than for the normal curve
          • → the confidence interval is stretched more to account for not knowing $\sigma$, the population SD.
  • If the sample size is large
    • → a large degree of freedom
      • → the $t$ distribution is almost identical to the normal
        • → there is no need to stretch further the confidence intervals.

Below is the same simulation as in (4.1), except that we use $t$ quantiles instead.

    sample = distr[key].rvs(n)

    # Get its sample mean and SE = SD/sqrt(n)
    sample_mean = sample.mean()
    sample_sem = stats.sem(sample)

    # Use CI with the sample SD but t quantile
    ci_lower, ci_upper = (sample_mean - t*sample_sem, sample_mean + t*sample_sem)

    # Keep track on whether the interval contains indeed or not the population mean
    if (mu >= ci_lower) and (mu <= ci_upper):
        ax[i].plot([k,k], [ci_lower, ci_upper], c='dodgerblue', lw=2, zorder=3)
        inside += 1
    else:
        ax[i].plot([k,k], [ci_lower, ci_upper], c='gray', lw=2, zorder=3)
        outside += 1

6. The bottom line¶

On whether to use $z$ vs $t$ quantiles (first vs second CI formula), it boils down to an educated choice that depends on

  • How large is your sample size $n$
  • How skewed is your data
Data is symmetric Data is somewhat skewed
Sample size $n$ is large Choice does not matter Choice does not matter (but go with $t$ to be safe)
Sample size $n$ is small Use $t$ quantiles $t$ is a not thaaat bad of choice

If your data is noticeably skewed and your sample size is particularly small, then the $t$ quantiles will not fix your problems.

Hope is not totally lost, but you'll have to be much more careful on how you analyze such data. The techniques involved often rely on additional information, like "Do you have a good guess on what the underlying distribution might be?" and/or you have get creative with bootstrapping.
