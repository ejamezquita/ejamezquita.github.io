Checking for normality and homoscedasticity

Remember that the end goal is to apply Tukey's honestly significant difference (HSD) test to check if oxygen consumption changes significantly under different water temperatures. For that test to be meaningful, we must check that for every temperature:

  1. The oxygen consumption data is normally distributed.
  2. The variance is the same.

While there is a variety of statistical tests, we should check visually that the two assumptions above make sense for the data at hand.

Attempt 1: Checking for normality with histograms

In principle, one way to visually check if the data follows a distribution is with histograms: compute the histogram of the data and then compare its shape to a known probability density function. If the histogram and the pdf share similar shapes, there is a good chance that the data is distributed as the pdf.

However, the shape of the histogram can vary depending on the number and positions of bins used. This makes the histogram eye-test quite unreliable.

Attempt 2: Checking for normality with Q-Q plots

A much better strategy is to use a Q-Q plot: compare the quantiles of our data versus the quantiles of a known distribution. If they follow a linear relationship, then chances are that the data follows that distribution. Remember that one way to check linearity is with Pearson's correlation index.

Summary

The study investigates the effects of thermal acclimation on the metabolic responses of two serpulid species, Spirobranchus spinosus and S. cf. corniculatus, highlighting how temperature influences oxygen consumption and ammonia excretion rates. Results indicate that the warm-water species S. cf. corniculatus experienced a decrease in oxygen consumption at elevated temperatures, while both species relied on protein catabolism for energy. This research provides foundational data on the metabolic responses of these tubeworms and enhances understanding of their adaptability to climate change.

  • The study focuses on the metabolic responses of two serpulid species, Spirobranchus spinosus and S. cf. corniculatus, to thermal acclimation in different temperature environments (temperate Northern Pacific and tropical Eastern Pacific).
  • Temperature significantly affects the physiology and metabolic responses of serpulids, influencing their growth, survival, and distribution patterns.
  • After a 30-day acclimation period at varying temperatures, oxygen consumption rates (OCR) and ammonia excretion rates (AER) were measured, revealing a decrease in OCR for the warm-water species S. cf. corniculatus at 33C.
  • The low O:N values (0.5-3.9) for both species indicate that they rely on protein catabolism for energy, highlighting their metabolic responses to environmental changes, such as ocean warming, and their potential ability to cope with climate change.
